## Anatomy Overview

### The Hip Flexor Muscles
| Muscle | Origin | Insertion | Function |
|--------|--------|-----------|----------|
| **Psoas Major** | T12-L5 vertebrae | Lesser trochanter of femur | Hip flexion, external rotation, spinal flexion |
| **Iliacus** | Iliac fossa | Lesser trochanter of femur | Hip flexion |
| **Rectus Femoris** | AIIS, acetabulum | Patella (via quad tendon) | Hip flexion, knee extension |
| **Sartorius** | ASIS (hip bone) | Pes anserinus (medial tibia) | Hip flexion, abduction, external rotation |
| **TFL (Tensor Fasciae Latae)** | ASIS, iliac crest | IT band | Hip flexion, abduction, internal rotation |
| **Pectineus** | Pectineal line of pubis | Pectineal line of femur | Hip flexion, adduction |

### Iliopsoas = Psoas Major + Iliacus
The iliopsoas is the primary hip flexor and one of the strongest muscles in the body. It's involved in:
- Walking and running
- Climbing stairs
- Standing from seated position
- Core stability (psoas attaches to spine)

### Important Note: Most People Have TIGHT Hip Flexors
Prolonged sitting shortens the hip flexors, leading to:
- Anterior pelvic tilt
- Lower back pain
- Inhibited glutes ("gluteal amnesia")

**Balance strengthening with stretching** for optimal hip health.

## Hanging Leg Raise Variations

### HF001 - Hanging Leg Raise (Straight Leg)
| Attribute | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Equipment** | Pull-up bar |
| **Difficulty** | Intermediate |
| **Primary** | Hip flexors (iliopsoas) |
| **Secondary** | Abs (lower), grip |
| **Contraindications** | Shoulder issues, lower back pain |

**Form Cues:**
- Hang from bar, arms straight
- Keep legs straight or slightly bent
- Raise legs until parallel to floor (or higher)
- Control the descent - don't swing
- Engage core throughout

**Common Mistakes:**
- Swinging/using momentum → Control the movement
- Rounding lower back excessively → Maintain slight neutral spine
- Stopping too low → Raise to at least 90 degrees

## Form Guides for Key Exercises

### Hanging Leg Raise - Detailed Form
1. **Grip:** Overhand, shoulder-width on pull-up bar
2. **Starting Position:** Arms straight, body hanging
3. **Initiate:** Engage abs, flex hips
4. **Raise:** Lift legs to at least parallel (90 degrees)
5. **Peak:** Pause briefly at top
6. **Lower:** Control descent, don't swing
7. **Breathing:** Exhale on the way up

### Mountain Climbers - Detailed Form
1. **Start:** High plank position, hands under shoulders
2. **Drive:** Bring knee toward chest
3. **Switch:** Alternate legs quickly
4. **Hips:** Keep them level, don't pike up
5. **Core:** Stay engaged throughout
6. **Speed:** Control speed based on goal (strength vs cardio)

### L-Sit - Detailed Form
1. **Position:** Hands on bars or floor, arms straight
2. **Lift:** Raise legs parallel to floor
3. **Lock:** Legs straight, point toes
4. **Hold:** Maintain position for time
5. **Shoulders:** Depressed (push down through hands)
6. **Core:** Engaged throughout

## Common Mistakes & Fixes

| Mistake | Why It's Wrong | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Swinging on hanging raises | Uses momentum, not muscles | Control the movement |
| Arching lower back (lying) | Strain on lumbar spine | Press lower back into floor |
| Not going high enough | Limits hip flexor activation | Raise to at least 90 degrees |
| Neglecting stretching | Tight hip flexors cause issues | Stretch after strengthening |
| Too much momentum | Reduces muscle activation | Slow, controlled reps |
| Holding breath | Increases blood pressure | Breathe steadily |

## Importance of Hip Flexor Health

### Signs of Tight/Weak Hip Flexors
1. Lower back pain (especially standing)
2. Anterior pelvic tilt
3. Hip "snapping" or clicking
4. Difficulty with deep squats
5. Poor running mechanics

### Protocol for Healthy Hip Flexors
1. **Strengthen** 2-3x/week with controlled exercises
2. **Stretch** daily, especially after sitting
3. **Activate glutes** to balance hip flexor dominance
4. **Avoid excessive sitting** when possible
5. **Warm up** hip flexors before running/sprinting
